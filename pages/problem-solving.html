Problem Solving

Discuss when you were blocked on a simple problem

Last week I felt blocked on many occasions. As I told my facilitator, I felt like I had been thrown into a deep pool and couldn't swim....and sharks were circling.

My biggest block this week was based around the GradeBook challenge. Trying to push the value of the new score into the testScores array, I firstly over-complicated the function by adding in a loop. I made the assumption that by adding a new score you would have to loop over the name key to able to target that array. To solve the problem, I Googled the solution, compared three or more sites that explained the push() method and tried to establish its syntax. I then set up a separate js & html page in VSCode to practice and troubleshoot the errors in the console.

I also did a bit more research on dot and bracket notation..."bracket notation is another way to access a property of an object". Throughout the process I kept on feeling frustrated. I chose to take breaks and sleep on the problem if it was taking too long.

This week however was very different to last week. Instead of getting overwhelmed and letting a fixed mindset creep in, I chose to stay positive, relaxed and nurture my growth mindset mentality.

A simple problem I solved in an elegant way was achieved by completing the deBuzz challenge. Instead of adding in each variant of the word buzz to filter (buzz, BUzz, buZZ - which I did initially), I converted the word toLowerCase.

Reflect on how confident you feel using each of these problem solving techniques/processes:

Pseudocode

This week I wasn't that confident writing pseudocode code. What I have realised about my learning process is that I tend to rush in with test code before spending time to work out the problem step by step. This week I tried to spend more time reflecting on the problem before jumping in.

Trying something

I feel confident trying anything off the bat (yes I like cricket). I tend to jump in head first and see what happens, this can be a positive or a negative process. It can sometimes lead me down a wild goose chase for an hour or more.

Rubber ducky method

This is a new technique I'm coming to grips with. I have been using my partner as a bouncing board to talk to and found it has helped a lot. I want to become more comfortable in vocalising my challenges publically and not keep those thoughts internally.

Reading error messages

I feel comfortable reading errors messages and this has formed a large part of my problem solving arsenal this week. I have tried to strip each problem down to simpler elements and see if they throw any errors. Baby steps!

Console.logging

I had a revelation this week and realised how useful and important console.logging was! I will be using it a lot more in the future to test my code to see if I'm on the right track. In fact, I used it a lot this week along with taking the baby steps above.

Googling

I Google a lot and tend to find myself down a few rabbit holes with too many open tabs. This is one of my primary problem solving techniques which I find most useful. However, I need to remember to stay focused on one task at a time and limit my Googling time to 'x' minutes.

Asking your peers for help/Asking coaches for help

I'm not the greatest at asking for help from my peers or coach. This is something I'm actively working on and will try to improve on during Sprint 5. I tend to battle on through, thinking surely the answer is just around the corner! I appreciate my peers who do step forward and help in any way they can. This inspires me to be more vocal moving forward... a problem shared is a problem halved.

Improving your process with reflection

Reflecting with my partner has been very valuable. Spending time discussing my work process has given me clarity to understand what I do and how I do it. I've learnt through reflection, I tend to rush in, want to fix the problem quickly and find a solution. Thats not a bad thing though, right?
